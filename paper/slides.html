The Problem

Three threats meet in one protocol

  • Updates leak. FL never shares raw data, but gradient-inversion attacks reconstruct training samples from individual model updates.
  • Secure aggregation fixes this: pairwise masks cancel on summation, so the server learns only the sum of updates.
  • A quantum computer breaks the key exchange underneath (Shor), and "harvest now, decrypt later" makes recorded traffic vulnerable today.
  • Post-quantum FL exists, but it is lattice-only: lattice secret sharing, lattice key-homomorphic PRFs, Dilithium signature aggregation.
  • One assumption to break them all: a single cryptanalytic advance against lattices defeats every such deployment at once.

The Idea

Cryptographic diversity, brought into FL

  • March 2025: NIST selects HQC, a code-based KEM whose security rests on syndrome decoding (not lattices), as the diversification backup to ML-KEM.
  • CB-SAFE: to our knowledge, the first FL secure-aggregation framework whose post-quantum confidentiality is code-based.
  • The KEM is one pluggable module: HQC, ML-KEM, or Kyber run under the identical masking protocol.
  • Plus a second axis: we reconcile secure aggregation with Byzantine robustness and quantify the exact price of doing both.
  • Everything is measured, not modeled: real liboqs crypto on the wire path; every byte and millisecond from running code.

The Protocol

KEM-agnostic cluster masking

  • One-time setup: every client publishes a KEM public key; pairwise secrets are established by encapsulation, once.
  • Every round r: mask seeds come from HKDF(ssij, r), so no KEM material ever crosses the wire again.
yi = q(xi) + G(bir) + Σj>i G(H(ssij, r)) − Σj<i G(H(ssij, r))   (mod 264)
  • Clusters of c clients: masks cancel within a cluster, so the server sees only cluster sums, never an individual update.
  • Dropouts: Shamir t-of-c recovery of self-masks; a cluster below threshold is excluded gracefully, never stalls the round.

Privacy Guarantee

Theorem 1: nothing beyond the cluster sum

  • Simulation-based proof: the honest-but-curious server, colluding with up to t−1 members of a cluster, sees a view computable from the cluster sum alone.
  • Hybrid argument: IND-CCA2 KEM → uniform pairwise secrets → PRG masks → submissions uniform subject to the revealed sum.
  • Anonymity set = the cluster's alive member count; both dropout-recovery paths are simulatable.
  • Defense adds zero leakage: CB-SAFE+ flags are post-processing of already-revealed sums plus server-local data.
  • Scope stated honestly: honest-but-curious server; where signatures are needed we compose ML-DSA, so only the KEM layer is code-based.

Result 1 · Cost of Diversity

The code-based premium is a one-time constant

  • Per-round traffic is identical for all six KEMs: 2,277 KiB per client, dominated by the masked update itself.
  • Setup: HQC-128 costs 15.2 KiB vs ML-KEM-512's 3.8 KiB per client (4.0×).
  • Under 0.03% of total traffic across 40 training rounds.
  • Compute: 2.0 s one-time for 30 clients vs 0.02 s; ~26 ms masking per round.
Even HQC-256's setup is ~2% of a single round's traffic (log scale).

Result 2 · Utility

Privacy provably costs no accuracy

  • 59.0% on non-IID CIFAR-10 (Dirichlet α=0.5, 30 clients, 40 rounds).
  • Secure sums match plain FedAvg in every round under both HQC and ML-KEM: max error 2.28×10−5, exactly the fixed-point bound.
  • Dropout recovery demonstrated live: cluster exclusion (round 10) and full Shamir + seed-reveal recovery (round 20).
  • Crypto is invisible next to training: ~30 ms masking vs ~21 s of local training per round.
One curve serves all three configurations: the aggregates are numerically equal.

The Tension

Hide vs inspect: a dial with an exchange rate

  • Robust rules need to inspect updates; secure aggregation hides them.
  • CB-SAFE's resolution is structural: privacy below the cluster boundary, robustness above it, over k cluster means.
  • The price: a cluster sum is clean only with probability (1−f)c.
  • Theory meets data: observed clean fractions 0.70 / 0.50 / 0.40 vs predicted 0.73 / 0.51 / 0.34.
Bigger anonymity sets leave fewer clean cluster sums for any robust rule.

Result 3 · The Laundering Effect

Cluster averaging disguises what it contaminates

  • One γ=5 sign-flipper in a cluster of 3: the cluster mean has exactly honest magnitude with inverted sign (γ* = 2c/m − 1).
  • Poisoned sums are not outliers — coordinate-wise rules see a ±h cloud that averages to zero.
  • Trimmed mean and median: pinned at 10.0% for f ≥ 20%. Multi-Krum (vector selection) survives: 53 / 43 / 17%.
  • The privacy mechanism structurally weakens exactly the defenses practitioners reach for.
Collapse occurs exactly at the contamination boundary the (1−f)c law predicts.

Result 4 · CB-SAFE+

Identifying attackers the server never sees

  • Re-randomized clusters every round + a server loss probe (200-sample root set): flag any cluster whose sum raises root loss.
  • Suspicion accumulates: an attacker sits in a flagged cluster nearly every round; an honest client only when randomly co-clustered (probability 1−(1−f)c−1).
  • Temporal group testing: the pools are privacy-preserving cluster sums; the tests repeat across training rounds; exclusion after a 5-round warmup.
  • Identification: 3/3 attackers at f=10%, 8/9 at f=30% — zero honest false positives in every configuration.
  • 42.8% accuracy at f=30% where every static rule reads 10–17% — the privacy cost refunded in rounds, not anonymity.

Result 5 · The Dial, Measured — and Honest Limits

What privacy costs, and where defenses end

  • c=1 (no privacy): median degrades gently, 50→39%. c=3 breaks past f=10%; c=5 already at f=10%.
  • At f=5%, privacy is free: private configs match the no-privacy baseline.
  • Honest limit: backdoor ASR stays 93–97% for every rule including ours — stealthy targeted attacks evade all sum-level probes.
  • Multi-Krum is stronger at f=20% (44% vs 37.6%): the defense's edge grows with attack severity; the two are complementary.
The breakdown frontier shifts left with cluster size, exactly as (1−f)c predicts.

Conclusion

Takeaways & what's next

  • Cryptographic diversity in FL is practically free: one pluggable KEM module, a setup-only premium under 0.03% of traffic.
  • Secure aggregation launders poisoning: defending requires signals that survive aggregation (direction, loss), accumulated over time.
  • CB-SAFE+ identifies individual attackers from group observations at zero additional privacy leakage.
  • Open problems: targeted backdoors under secure aggregation, actively malicious servers, adaptive attackers gaming the probe margin.
  • In progress: multi-seed replication with significance tests, FashionMNIST, 100-client scale — full implementation released open-source.
